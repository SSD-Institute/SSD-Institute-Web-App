Multi-Choice Voting

Definition: Multi-choice voting permits citizens to cast votes for every candidate they support rather than being limited to a single selection. This approach eliminates the "spoiler effect" and reduces the need for strategic voting by allowing a broader expression of voter preference. The candidate with the highest total support wins, more accurately reflecting the collective will of the electorate.

How It Works

In a traditional "choose-one" election, you are forced to pick a single person, even if you like several candidates or feel like you are choosing the "lesser of two evils." With multi-choice voting, if five people are running, you can check the box for one, three, or even all five of them (but only once per candidate).

The candidate with the most votes wins, even if they don't have a majority. This means that if you like three candidates, you can vote for all three without worrying about splitting the vote and accidentally helping your least favorite candidate win.

This system is designed to solve the "spoiler effect." This happens in standard elections when two similar candidates "split" the votes of their supporters, accidentally allowing a candidate that the majority of people dislike to win. Because you can support multiple people under this system, you don't have to worry about "wasting" your vote on an underdog.

Why This Matters

Multi-choice voting allows for a more accurate representation of voter preferences. It encourages candidates to appeal to a broader base of voters, rather than just trying to be the "lesser evil." This can lead to more positive campaigning and a more diverse range of candidates running for office.

Encouraging participation in elections is another key benefit. When voters understand they can support multiple candidates, they are more likely to vote, especially if they believe their preferred candidate has a chance to win.

More accurate representation of voter preferences can lead to better policy outcomes. When elected officials know they need to appeal to a broader base, they may be more likely to adopt policies that reflect the interests of a wider range of constituents.

The above simulations use real historical U.S. presidential election results as a baseline and then model how those same elections might have unfolded under an Approval-style Multi-Choice voting system. In the 2000 United States presidential election, official popular vote totals from the Federal Election Commission are used for George W. Bush, Al Gore, and Ralph Nader. The simulated approval totals reflect established political science findings indicating that many Nader voters preferred Gore over Bush in two-way preference polling. The purpose of this modeled dataset is not to rewrite history, but to demonstrate how allowing voters to support more than one candidate could have captured broader coalition preferences and reduced vote-splitting dynamics.

The 1992 United States presidential election provides a second test case. Official popular vote totals for Bill Clinton, George H. W. Bush, and Ross Perot are used as the historical reference. The approval simulation models cross-support among Perot voters, who research shows were ideologically mixed and not uniformly aligned with either major party. The adjusted totals demonstrate how approval voting measures overall acceptability rather than forcing exclusive choice.

Finally, the 1824 United States presidential election is included as a structural example. The original election resulted in no candidate receiving an electoral majority, sending the decision to the House of Representatives. The simulated approval totals are modeled based on documented ideological alliances among Andrew Jackson, John Quincy Adams, William H. Crawford, and Henry Clay. Because national popular vote reporting in 1824 was incomplete and varied by state, these approval totals are illustrative rather than reconstructive. The intent is to demonstrate how approval-style voting could reduce contingent elections by measuring broader support directly.

Across all three cases, the historical vote totals come from official archival records, while the approval totals are explicitly modeled to illustrate structural differences in outcome measurement.
